Tero is telemetry control.

After you connect your observability stack, Tero shows the state of your telemetry estate: what exists, which issues need review, which policies can fix them, where those policies run, and what impact they have.

Tero helps you control telemetry policy. It finds issues across cost, compliance, and signal quality. Each issue carries evidence, provenance, impact, and a recommended policy or action. You review the evidence before you decide what should change.

Policies are control-plane artifacts. Tero presents them with status, source, spec, related issue, deployment state, and measured activity. The control plane owns durable policy state; your providers, repositories, collectors, and Edge instances run the changes.

In practice, you use Tero this way:

1. [Issues](/issues/overview): Tero shows what it found and why it matters.
2. [Policies](/policies): you review the policy that would address the issue.
3. Runtime: Tero shows where the policy runs, including provider configuration and [Edge instances](/edge/overview).
4. Impact: Tero measures whether the issue, cost, or compliance exposure changed after action.

Tero does not replace your observability provider. Datadog, Splunk, collectors, repositories, and Edge remain the systems where telemetry lives and changes run. Tero sits above them as the control layer between evidence and action.

## Why telemetry needs control

Telemetry grows through local decisions. A team adds a debug log during an incident. A health check fires once per second forever. A field duplicates another field because two libraries use different names. Each change can make sense in isolation, but the estate becomes expensive and harder to trust.

Pipelines can move or transform data. Providers can store and query it. Tero adds the missing control loop: find the issue, show the evidence, propose the policy, track runtime state, and measure the result.
